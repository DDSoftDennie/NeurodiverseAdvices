What is Neurodiversity?

Neurodiversity refers to the natural variation in human brain function and cognitive traits. It includes autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette syndrome, and more. These are not deficits â€” they are different ways of experiencing and interacting with the world.

For Supporters, Carers & Co-workers

Practical, research-backed guidance for everyone who lives or works alongside a neurodiverse person.

Research shows that clear, direct communication dramatically reduces anxiety and misunderstandings for autistic and ADHD individuals.

  • Use clear, literal language and avoid idioms, sarcasm, or ambiguous phrases unless you explain them.
  • Give one instruction at a time; chunk larger tasks into numbered steps.
  • Allow processing time â€” a pause after speaking does not mean they are not listening.
  • Prefer written communication (email, chat) for complex information so they can re-read it.
  • Ask for a preferred communication style early; never assume.
  • Avoid interrupting mid-thought, especially during "info-dumps" which are a natural sharing style for many autistic people.
  • Confirm understanding by asking them to summarise back in their own words â€” not "did you understand?"

Sensory processing differences affect up to 90% of autistic individuals and are common in ADHD. Environmental adjustments have a significant impact on wellbeing and performance.

  • Reduce fluorescent or flickering lighting; offer natural or warm-toned alternatives.
  • Minimise background noise â€” provide noise-cancelling headphones or a quiet workspace.
  • Avoid strong scents (perfumes, cleaning products) in shared spaces.
  • Allow flexible seating or standing options, and respect clothing/texture preferences.
  • Create a designated calm/retreat space where overwhelm can be managed privately.
  • Maintain predictable routines and give advance notice of changes.
  • Ask the individual what specific sensory challenges they face â€” it varies greatly.

Meltdowns and shutdowns are neurological responses to sensory or emotional overload â€” not behaviour choices or manipulation.

  • During a meltdown: stay calm, speak softly or not at all, reduce sensory input, give space.
  • During a shutdown: do not force interaction â€” offer presence without demand, and check in gently later.
  • Never punish or shame after a meltdown; the person is already distressed and often embarrassed.
  • Identify triggers together during calm periods and build an agreed "early warning system".
  • Learn their individual signs of overload before the point of no return.
  • Teach and practise regulation strategies together â€” deep pressure, cold water on wrists, breathing techniques.
  • Recovery time is real and needed â€” do not rush a return to tasks.

Low-cost, high-impact adjustments can dramatically improve performance and wellbeing without requiring a formal diagnosis in many workplaces.

  • Provide written agendas for meetings ahead of time.
  • Allow flexible start times or remote work where possible (executive function is highly variable).
  • Give extended time for tasks or exams without drawing attention to it publicly.
  • Use visual schedules, checklists, and calendars as standard tools.
  • Avoid open-plan spaces for focused work; offer quiet zones or pods.
  • Offer body-doubling options (working silently alongside someone) â€” research shows this improves ADHD focus.
  • Assign one named point-of-contact for navigating complex systems (HR, school admin).
  • Focus performance assessments on outcomes, not on neurotypical-style process adherence.

Autistic burnout is a recognised condition driven by prolonged masking and unsupportive environments â€” not inherent fragility.

  • Recognise the signs of masking (performing neurotypicality) â€” exhaustion, withdrawal, increased stimming after social events.
  • Explicitly communicate that masking is not expected or required around you.
  • Ensure they have protected downtime with no social obligations.
  • Validate experiences without minimising: "That sounds really hard" beats "Everyone feels like that."
  • Encourage connection with other neurodivergent people â€” peer support has strong evidence of benefit.
  • Be aware that depression and anxiety co-occur with ADHD and autism at far higher rates than in the general population.
  • Support access to neurodiversity-affirming therapists (look for those who avoid ABA-based or normalising approaches).

Connection-first parenting approaches consistently outperform compliance-focused methods for neurodiverse children's long-term wellbeing.

  • Use the Pathological Demand Avoidance (PDA) lens if your child is highly demand-avoidant â€” traditional ABA and rewards/punishments often backfire.
  • Prioritise relationship over compliance: a child who trusts you will cooperate more over time.
  • Celebrate special interests genuinely â€” they support learning, identity, and emotional regulation.
  • Advocate loudly at school: request an Education Health and Care (EHC) plan or 504 plan and review it annually.
  • Model and teach interoception (identifying internal feelings) â€” this is often underdeveloped and leads to emotional dysregulation.
  • Avoid verbal praise overload ("good job!") â€” prefer specific, factual observations ("you waited your turn three times, that was hard").
  • Look after your own mental health; carer wellbeing directly affects the child's outcomes.

Good intentions with poor methods can cause lasting harm. Awareness helps everyone do better.

  • Forcing eye contact â€” causes pain and anxiety for many autistic people and has no social benefit for them.
  • Saying "you don't look autistic" â€” invalidates their experience; autism is invisible by design in many people who mask heavily.
  • Stopping stimming â€” stimming is a self-regulation tool; suppressing it increases anxiety and is linked to trauma.
  • Praising masking â€” telling someone they are "doing so well" while they are masking reinforces harmful behaviour.
  • Using functioning labels (high-/low-functioning) â€” oversimplifies and is discouraged by autistic self-advocacy groups.
  • Overloading with therapy â€” multiple hours of structured therapy per week without downtime leads to burnout and trauma.
  • Assuming they don't notice â€” neurodiverse people are often hyperaware of social dynamics; dismissiveness is always registered.

For Neurodiverse Individuals

Strategies, tools, and self-knowledge grounded in the latest neurodiversity-affirming research â€” written for you.

The neurodiversity energy model ("spoon theory" or "battery model") recognises that cognitive and social tasks cost you more energy than they cost neurotypical people.

  • Track your energy with a simple daily log â€” notice what drains and what restores you.
  • Protect non-negotiable recharge time after high-demand events (no guilt required).
  • Build in transition time between activities; rapid context-switching is especially costly.
  • Identify your "easy wins" â€” tasks or contexts that reliably feel energising â€” and schedule these when depleted.
  • Say no strategically: declining a social obligation early costs less energy than masking through it.
  • Use stimming freely in private spaces â€” research confirms it reduces anxiety and improves focus.
  • Batch difficult tasks with your peak cognitive window (know whether you are a morning or evening person â€” ADHD often involves delayed sleep phase).

ADHD is a difference in dopamine and noradrenaline regulation, not a willpower deficit. Strategies that work with your brain's reward system are far more effective than willpower-based approaches.

  • Use body doubling â€” working alongside another person (even on video) increases focus for many with ADHD.
  • Externalise your working memory: write everything down immediately, use visual cues, leave objects in sight as reminders.
  • Break tasks into the smallest possible units and start with just two minutes.
  • Use timers and the Pomodoro technique (25 min on, 5 min off) to make time concrete and visible.
  • Exercise before demanding cognitive tasks â€” it temporarily raises dopamine and noradrenaline levels.
  • Pair boring tasks with something enjoyable (background music, podcast, walking).
  • Acknowledge hyperfocus as a superpower and a risk â€” set external alarms to transition out of it.
  • Consider medication evaluation â€” stimulant medications have the strongest evidence base of any psychiatric medication.

Masking (camouflaging autistic traits to appear neurotypical) is linked to exhaustion, depression, anxiety, and a delayed sense of identity. Reducing masking is a key goal of autistic wellbeing.

  • Notice when you are masking â€” facial expressions "performed", suppressed stimming, scripted responses â€” and ask yourself if it is truly necessary.
  • Find at least one safe space or person where you can be unmasked, even briefly.
  • Give yourself permission to stim in public â€” it is a human neurological need, not a sign of disorder.
  • Seek out autistic community (online forums, local groups) â€” peer connection is protective against depression.
  • Recognise that late diagnosis is common, especially in women and non-binary people (historically under-diagnosed). Your experiences are valid regardless of formal diagnosis.
  • Process past experiences with a neurodiversity-affirming therapist â€” many autistic adults carry significant grief around late diagnosis.
  • Reframe your traits as a different operating system, not a broken one.

Dyslexia, dyscalculia, dyspraxia, and related differences reflect alternative cognitive profiles â€” often including remarkable strengths in spatial reasoning, creative thinking, and holistic processing.

  • Use text-to-speech tools (Natural Reader, built-in OS readers) to access written material without decoding effort.
  • Use speech-to-text for writing tasks â€” removes the transcription bottleneck so ideas can flow.
  • Try coloured overlays or background colours when reading (some people find cream or pale yellow reduces visual stress).
  • Use mind maps and visual diagrams instead of linear note-taking.
  • For maths: use manipulatives, calculators, and visual representations freely â€” arithmetic fluency is not the same as mathematical ability.
  • Break reading tasks into smaller chunks and use a finger or ruler to track lines.
  • Leverage your strengths: dyslexic thinkers are statistically over-represented in entrepreneurship, art, and engineering.

Anxiety and depression affect around 50% of autistic adults and 40% of those with ADHD â€” far above population rates. These are largely environmentally driven and highly treatable.

  • Distinguish between sensory-driven anxiety and generalised anxiety â€” they require different responses.
  • Recognise autistic burnout early: persistent exhaustion, loss of skills, emotional flatness after sustained effort.
  • Prioritise sleep rigorously â€” sleep disruption exacerbates every other symptom dramatically.
  • Use structured routines as an anxiety-management tool, not just for productivity.
  • Practice interoceptive awareness â€” body scanning, noticing hunger, temperature, tension â€” to build emotional awareness.
  • Seek neurodiversity-affirming therapy (ACT and CBT adapted for autism have the strongest evidence base).
  • Connect with others who understand: online communities (Autistic Self Advocacy Network, ADHD subreddits) offer validation that is often unavailable in daily life.

Many neurodiverse people thrive professionally when roles align with their cognitive strengths. Disclosure, self-advocacy, and the right environment are key.

  • Identify roles that leverage your strengths: pattern recognition, hyperfocus, creative thinking, systemising, detail orientation.
  • Consider disclosure carefully â€” many workplaces have legal obligations to provide reasonable adjustments; knowing your rights empowers you.
  • Request accommodations in writing and frame them in terms of what helps you produce your best work.
  • Find an ally or mentor at work who understands your working style.
  • Know your "shutdown" conditions at work and have an agreed exit plan with a trusted colleague.
  • Build structure into your day even if no one else does â€” calendar blocking, morning routines, and end-of-day reviews all reduce decision fatigue.
  • Explore neurodiversity-focused employment programmes (e.g. SAP Autism at Work, Microsoft Neurodiversity Hiring Programme).

Many neurodiverse people have rich, deep friendships and relationships â€” but social energy is finite and social rules can be genuinely confusing. Being explicit helps.

  • Prioritise quality over quantity in friendships â€” a few deep connections are far better for wellbeing than many shallow ones.
  • Be honest about your needs: "I need 24 hours' notice to change plans" is a reasonable request, not a character flaw.
  • Ask for explicit communication from others â€” "Can you tell me directly when something bothers you?"
